Korean marks the **topic** of a sentence (은/는) and the **subject** (이/가) with different particles. English doesn't, which is why this trips most learners up early.

## The pairs

- **은 / 는** — topic marker. Use 은 after a consonant, 는 after a vowel.
- **이 / 가** — subject marker. Use 이 after a consonant, 가 after a vowel.

## When to use which

| Use 은/는 (topic) | Use 이/가 (subject) |
|---|---|
| Introducing or contrasting a topic | Identifying *which* thing does the verb |
| Known / already-mentioned information | New information, the answer to "who?" or "what?" |
| "As for X, …" | "It is X that …" |

## Examples

- 저는 마사예요. — *As for me, I'm Mahsa.* (introducing the topic "me")
- 누가 왔어요? — *Who came?* → 마사가 왔어요. — *Mahsa came.* (이/가 because we're identifying who)
- 김치는 매워요. — *Kimchi (as a topic) is spicy.* (general statement about kimchi)
- 이 김치가 매워요. — *This kimchi is spicy.* (specifically this one)

## Rule of thumb

If you can prepend *"as for…"* or *"speaking of…"* in English, use 은/는. If you'd stress the noun with your voice in English ("**Mahsa** came"), use 이/가.
