## The problem

Real data has too many columns. A patient record might have 300 lab values, an image might have a million pixels, a survey might have 80 questions. Most of those columns are correlated ,  they're partial views of the same underlying thing. We want to keep the underlying thing and throw away the redundancy.

Plot height and weight for a hundred people. You get a cloud that stretches diagonally ,  tall people tend to be heavier. The cloud is "really" one-dimensional (a "size" axis) with a bit of noise around it. PCA is the procedure that finds that axis.

## The core idea: maximum variance

Look at the cloud. Imagine drawing a line through it. Project every point onto that line (drop a perpendicular). You now have a 1D summary of the data. Some lines give a good summary ,  points spread out along them. Other lines give a useless one ,  all the points squish near the middle.

The first principal component is the direction along which the projected points have the **largest variance**. It's the "long axis" of the cloud ,  the direction that loses the least information when you collapse to 1D.

## Adding more components

The second principal component is the direction of greatest *remaining* variance, with the constraint that it must be perpendicular to PC1. The third is perpendicular to both PC1 and PC2. And so on.

For n-dimensional data you get up to n principal components. But that's not the point ,  the point is that the first few usually carry most of the variance, and the rest are mostly noise.

## How much does each component explain?

Each component comes with a number: the variance of the data projected onto it. Divide each by the total variance and you get the **explained variance ratio** ,  what fraction of the spread that component captures.

Plotted as a bar chart from largest to smallest, this is a **scree plot**. The interesting question is where the bars drop off ,  the "elbow." Components before the elbow describe real structure; components after are mostly noise.

## Reducing dimensions

Once you decide how many components to keep ,  say two ,  you project every data point onto those two directions. Each point goes from *n* numbers to two numbers. That's the dimensionality reduction.

Visually: take the 2D cloud above, rotate it so PC1 is horizontal, and you've turned a diagonal cloud into a roughly horizontal one. Now the x-axis is the meaningful axis and the y-axis is the small remaining noise.

## Interpreting the components

Each principal component is a weighted mix of the original features. Those weights are called **loadings**. Reading them tells you what each PC "means."

In the height-weight example, PC1 might be something like `0.7 × height + 0.7 × weight` ,  a general "size" axis. PC2 might be `0.7 × height − 0.7 × weight` ,  a "tall and skinny vs. short and stocky" axis. Same data, new vocabulary.

## A few things to remember

- **Center and scale first.** PCA is sensitive to units. Height in cm and weight in tonnes will produce very different components than height in metres and weight in kg. Standardise to zero mean and unit variance unless you have a strong reason not to.
- **PCA only finds linear structure.** If the data lies on a curve or a swiss-roll, PCA will fail to see it. Reach for t-SNE, UMAP, or autoencoders when the relationships aren't linear.
- **Components are not features you can interpret directly**, especially in high dimensions. They're mixtures. Loadings help, but you'll usually only be able to "read" the first two or three.
- **PCA doesn't know about labels.** It maximises variance, not class separability. For supervised dimensionality reduction, look at LDA or supervised variants of PCA.

## Takeaway

PCA is a rotation. It finds the direction of greatest spread, then the next greatest perpendicular direction, and so on. You keep the components that matter and drop the ones that are mostly noise. The result is fewer features that are uncorrelated and ordered by importance ,  easier to plot, faster to model, and less prone to overfitting.
